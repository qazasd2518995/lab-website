Repeated-measures ANOVA

重複量數變異數分析 · removing individual differences

Overview

When every subject experiences every condition, the within-subjects variance splits again into a conditions part and an error part, after the stable between-subjects differences are removed. Pulling out individual differences sharpens the test, which is why repeated-measures designs are so powerful.

Key formulas
$$SS_{\text{within-subj}} = SS_{\text{conditions}} + SS_{\text{error}}$$
$$F = \dfrac{MS_{\text{conditions}}}{MS_{\text{error}}}$$
$$\eta^2_{\text{partial}} = \dfrac{SS_{\text{conditions}}}{SS_{\text{conditions}} + SS_{\text{error}}}$$
Two-stage partition

Total variance is split twice. First, the stable between-subjects differences are set aside. What remains — the within-subjects variance — is then split into the conditions effect and the error against which it is tested.

ANOVA summary table

Five learners measured at three time points (weeks 1, 6, 12). The conditions effect is tested against error, not against the large between-subjects variance — which is why it is set aside in its own row.

The sphericity assumption

Repeated-measures designs carry one extra assumption beyond ordinary ANOVA: sphericity — the variances of the differences between every pair of conditions are equal. Mauchly's test checks it. When it fails (p < .05), apply a Greenhouse–Geisser (or Huynh–Feldt) correction, which multiplies the degrees of freedom by ε < 1 so the F-test stays valid.

Post-hoc · Bonferroni-corrected pairs

A significant omnibus test says the conditions differ somewhere. Pairwise comparisons with a Bonferroni correction control the family-wise error rate across the three contrasts.

When to use
  • The same subjects measured under several conditions or times.
  • Longitudinal or within-subject experimental designs.
  • When between-subject noise would otherwise mask the effect.
