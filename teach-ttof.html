From t to F · one view

從 t 到 F · the same signal-over-noise idea

Overview

The t-test and ANOVA are not rival procedures. With exactly two groups, the F-statistic equals the square of the t-statistic: t² = F. Both are a signal divided by an estimate of noise; ANOVA generalises the comparison to many groups at once. This is the lab's framework in miniature: structure first, then a ratio.

Key formulas
$$t^2 = F \quad (\text{for } k = 2 \text{ groups})$$
$$F = \dfrac{MS_{\text{between}}}{MS_{\text{within}}}$$
One dataset, two routes

Take two groups of n = 5. Group A = {8, 7, 9, 6, 10}, mean 8.0; Group B = {5, 4, 6, 3, 7}, mean 5.0. Each group has a sum of squares of 10, so the pooled variance is 2.50. Run it as a t-test and as an ANOVA, and the two answers lock together.

The t route
$$S_p^2 = \dfrac{SS_1 + SS_2}{n_1 + n_2 - 2} = \dfrac{10 + 10}{8} = 2.50$$
$$\mathrm{SE} = \sqrt{S_p^2\left(\tfrac{1}{n_1} + \tfrac{1}{n_2}\right)} = \sqrt{2.50 \cdot 0.4} = 1.00$$
$$t = \dfrac{\bar{X}_1 - \bar{X}_2}{\mathrm{SE}} = \dfrac{8.0 - 5.0}{1.00} = 3.000, \quad df = 8$$
The ANOVA route

Grand mean = 6.5. The between-groups sum of squares measures how far the group means sit from the grand mean; the within-groups sum of squares is the pooled scatter. Their mean squares form F.

Interactive F critical-value table

Beyond two groups the t-test runs out; ANOVA carries on. Look up the critical F for any numerator and denominator df here — for the example above, Fcrit(1, 8) at α = .05.

When to use
  • Seeing why a two-group ANOVA and a t-test agree.
  • Connecting the t-table and the F-table through degrees of freedom.
  • Framing all mean comparisons as signal over noise.
