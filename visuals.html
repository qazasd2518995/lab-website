VISUAL SUITE I  ·  № 01 / 2026

One dataset.
Six methodological lenses.

A simulated two-dimensional social-science dataset, rendered through the master operator and each of its decompositions.
N = 400 students K = 10 schools T = 5 occasions 2 variables · 6 views
— Note from the lab

The figures that follow take a single simulated joint distribution: student socioeconomic status (X) against academic achievement (Y), with nesting in schools and observation across time. Each figure illustrates what one axis of the master operator 𝓜 = 𝓛(Λ) ∘ 𝓗(Z, G) ∘ 𝓓(∂/∂t) brings into view. The same 400 points are read first as a pooled cloud, then as a hierarchy, then as cluster-specific covariance ellipses, then as a density field, then as a rotation onto principal axes, and finally as a dynamical flow. Each rendering is a coordinate. Together they describe what the literature too often summarizes as a single regression coefficient.

— Fig. 01

The multilevel reality, in two variables.

Four hundred students, ten schools, two variables. The pooled regression line cuts through a structure it cannot see. The between-school gradient is steeper than the within-school slopes that constitute it. This is Simpson's paradox in its quietest form.

Pooled OLS Yᵢ = β₀ + β₁ Xᵢ + εᵢ
— Fig. 02

Decomposing heterogeneity: within and between.

The same 400 points, now split into ten within-school regressions and ten school-mean coordinates. The between-school slope (2.77) far exceeds any within-school slope. A multilevel model that ignored this distinction would mis-attribute variance from school context to student-level SES.

Hierarchical form y = Xβ + Zu + ε  ,  u ∼ 𝒩(0, G)
— Fig. 03

Cluster-specific covariance, ten times over.

Each school carries its own bivariate covariance with a unique orientation, elongation, and location. The dashed pooled ellipse hides this variation. Methods that assume covariance homogeneity across clusters discard the very information multilevel SEM was built to recover.

Cluster covariance Σⱼ = Cov(Xⱼ, Yⱼ)  ,  j = 1, …, 10
— Fig. 04

The joint distribution as a density field.

A bivariate kernel density with hexbinned counts and contour lines, flanked by the two marginal distributions. The joint density f(X, Y) carries more information than either marginal alone, and considerably more than the correlation coefficient that often substitutes for it.

Joint density f(x, y) = ∂²F / ∂x ∂y
— Fig. 05

Rotation to principal axes. The matrix at work.

Eigendecomposition of the sample covariance matrix produces the orthogonal basis along which variance is maximized. PC1 absorbs roughly three-quarters of the joint variance. PC2 captures what remains. Matrix algebra is not an abstraction here. It is the physical rotation of the data into its own preferred coordinate system.

Spectral form Σ = V Λ V⊤  ,  Σ vₖ = λₖ vₖ
— Fig. 06

Latent growth in continuous time.

The same students, now observed across five occasions. Thin lines are individual trajectories. Thick lines are school means. The black trace is the grand mean. Heterogeneity in intercept and slope across persons and schools is the substantive content of any growth model, and the entry point for differential-equation formulations of change.

Latent growth ODE dY/dt = π₀ + π₁ t + ξ(t)
end of plates

The simulation, in plain terms.

Ten schools (j = 1, …, 10) drew random intercepts and slopes from a normal distribution. Within each school, forty students drew an SES value correlated with their school's intercept, and an achievement value generated as a school-specific linear function of SES with normal noise.

Each student also drew a personal growth rate, and was then "observed" at five evenly spaced time points with measurement error added at each occasion. The total simulation produced 2,000 observations from 400 persons in 10 clusters.

Generative equations.

School level β₀ⱼ ∼ 𝒩(0, 1.20²)  ,  β₁ⱼ ∼ 𝒩(0.60, 0.25²)
Student level (cross-sectional) Yᵢⱼ = 1.5 + β₀ⱼ + β₁ⱼ Xᵢⱼ + εᵢⱼ
Longitudinal extension Yᵢⱼ(t) = Yᵢⱼ(0) + ρᵢ · t + ξᵢⱼ(t)
Noise structure εᵢⱼ ∼ 𝒩(0, 0.7²)  ,  ξᵢⱼ(t) ∼ 𝒩(0, 0.3²)
