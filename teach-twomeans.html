Two means, six tests

兩平均數差 μ₁ − μ₂ · choosing the right denominator

Overview

Every two-sample test of \( \mu_1 - \mu_2 \) is one idea in different clothes. Three questions (is σ known? are the samples large? are the groups independent or paired?) pick one of six tests. The numerator is always the observed difference minus its null value; only the standard error in the denominator changes.

Key formulas
$$\text{statistic} = \dfrac{(\bar{X}_1 - \bar{X}_2) - (\mu_1 - \mu_2)}{\widehat{\mathrm{SE}}(\bar{X}_1 - \bar{X}_2)}$$
$$S_p^2 = \dfrac{(n_1-1)S_1^2 + (n_2-1)S_2^2}{n_1 + n_2 - 2}$$
The decision tree

Three questions, asked in order, route you to one of six tests. First: is the population σ known? If not, are the samples large enough for the normal approximation? And finally: are the two groups independent, or paired by design?

The six tests

Same numerator every time. What changes is the denominator — how the standard error of the difference is estimated.

One template, three forks

Every test is (observed difference − null difference) / SE. The numerator is the signal; only the way the denominator estimates noise changes. Three forks decide it: (i) σ known vs estimated picks z or t; (ii) assuming equal variances vs not picks pooled or Welch — modern practice leans Welch; (iii) independent vs paired is set by the design, and when paired, positive correlation shrinks the SE and buys precision.

When to use
  • Comparing two group means and unsure which test applies.
  • Deciding between pooled and Welch t under unequal variances.
  • Recognising paired designs: positive correlation buys precision.
