The paired-samples t-test

成對樣本 t 檢定 · pre/post, repeated measures, matched pairs

Overview

When the same subjects are measured twice, the two samples are correlated by design. Reduce the problem to a one-sample test on the differences \( D_i = X_{1i} - X_{2i} \). The within-pair correlation is absorbed automatically. Written with covariance, the term \( -2rS_1S_2 \) shrinks the standard error: positive correlation buys precision.

Key formulas
$$t = \dfrac{\bar{D} - \mu_D}{S_D/\sqrt{n}}, \quad df = n - 1$$
$$\widehat{\mathrm{SE}} = \sqrt{\dfrac{S_1^2 + S_2^2 - 2\,r\,S_1 S_2}{n}}$$
$$d = \dfrac{\bar{D}}{S_D}$$
The within-subject change

Each subject is measured before and after. A line connects that person's two scores — the paired test models the change along each line, not the gap between two separate clouds of people.

Worked example · vocabulary gain

Five learners take a vocabulary test before and after a six-week course. Work on the differences, not the two columns.

The seven-step computation
  1. Differences — compute Dᵢ = X₁ᵢ − X₂ᵢ for each pair.
  2. Mean difference — D̄ = ΣDᵢ / n.
  3. Spread — S_D, the standard deviation of the differences.
  4. Standard error — SE = S_D / √n.
  5. Statistic — t = D̄ / SE, under H₀: μ_D = 0.
  6. Degrees of freedom — df = n − 1.
  7. Effect size — d = D̄ / S_D, a within-subject Cohen's d.
Five common pitfalls
  • Running an independent t-test on paired data — it inflates the error term.
  • Using the wrong df: it is n − 1 (pairs), not n₁ + n₂ − 2.
  • Ignoring the sign of D̄ — a negative mean difference reverses the interpretation.
  • Checking normality of the raw scores instead of the differences.
  • Misreading the design: "two conditions, two scores per person" is paired.
When to use
  • Pre/post designs measuring the same people twice.
  • Matched-pairs or repeated single-factor comparisons.
  • Whenever ignoring the pairing would waste the design's power.
